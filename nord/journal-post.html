What washed processing really does to a cup

Of all the decisions that shape a coffee before it reaches a roaster, processing is among the most consequential — and the least understood. Washed processing, in particular, is often described as "clean", as though that were the whole story. It is not.

When cherry is washed, the fruit is removed before drying. The seed never sits in contact with its own sugars for long, which means the cup expresses the bean itself: the variety, the altitude, the soil. What you taste is the coffee, not the fruit it grew inside.

Clarity is the point

This is why washed coffees read as bright and articulate. A washed Yirgacheffe like our Konga shows jasmine and bergamot because nothing is sitting on top of those notes. The process gets out of the way — which is, in the end, the same principle we apply to roasting.

A washed coffee hides nothing. That is its risk and its reward.

It also means there is nowhere for a defect to hide. A poorly picked or under-ripe cherry will show as a flat, papery note that a natural process might mask under fermented fruit. So washed processing demands selective picking — and selective picking demands a price that rewards it.

What to look for

Brew a washed coffee as filter, slightly cooler than you might a darker roast, and let it cool in the cup. The acidity should feel structured rather than sharp, and the finish should be long and clean. If it is, the whole chain — grower, station, importer, roaster — did its job.
